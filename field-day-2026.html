The Location

An elevated, decommissioned Missouri fire lookout. Hauling gear, cables, and heavy lithium batteries up 100 feet of narrow iron stairs is the ultimate test of portable station engineering.

Field Day Summary: Our crew decided that "temporary QTH" meant "vertical QTH." We deployed to a decommissioned fire tower, hauling gear up 100 feet of stairs. The goal was simple: test our emergency setup, run on 100% off-grid power, and take advantage of the tower’s natural height for VHF and HF antenna support. Despite the cramped quarters in the metal cab, we made several rewarding contacts and had an unforgettable day.

Arrival & Assessment

Our home for the operation. The steel structure is rock solid, but that cab gets smaller and higher every single time you look up from the ground. Weather was on our side!

100-Foot Takeoff

The view from the top is breathtaking, overlooking dense, rolling Missouri forest for miles. This natural elevation gave us a fantastic takeoff angle and a massive RF advantage.

Operations Commenced

Setting up the primary station within the small dimensions of the tower cab. Maximizing limited workspace with efficient gear placement is half the battle.

The 20M Workhorse

Our main station setup centered on the rugged Yaesu FT-857. Here we are, dialed in and running strong on 20 meters SSB (14.337 MHz).

VHF/UHF Operations

Trey manning the 2M/70cm station. The tower’s extreme height made hitting distant repeaters and simplex contacts trivial, even with modest handheld setups. We even managed to snare one highly memorable contact via a high-altitude balloon repeater over Texas!

The Nerve Center

A closer look at our operating position desk. We deployed a Wio L1 MESH node to synchronize our logging and monitor network status off-grid. A healthy mess of cables is mandatory protocol for any real Field Day station!

Another View

Watching a front move in on the horizon. From this height, you can truly see for miles.

Scanning the Horizon

Another look out the alternate windows. The tower's 100-foot elevation gave us a commanding 360-degree line of sight in every direction.

Perspective

Looking straight down past the framework stairs toward our support vehicle. It served as a constant, stark reminder of why we triple-tied and anchored every single connector, handset, and hand tool!

Off-Grid Power Mess

We operated 100% on clean LiFePO4 battery power. While the parallel bus wiring layout is a bit chaotic to look at, it provided completely silent, ultra-steady current without voltage sag all weekend. Note the blue wire strippers on standby—always prepared!

Trey on HF

Trey hunting contacts on the HF SSB bands. Let's just say we quickly realized how much we wished we had brought dual-ear noise-isolating headphones to block out local noise in that cozy metal cab!

Will at the Station

Taking my turn at the microphone. Operating completely off-grid from an iconic, historic fire tower makes every single logged exchange feel incredibly satisfying.
