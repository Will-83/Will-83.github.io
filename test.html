Portable From the Ozark Canopy

Arrival & Assessment

Setting up a station at the top of a historic 100-foot watchtower requires careful planning. Every piece of gear—the Yaesu FT-857, heavy Bioenno LiFePO4 batteries, coax, ropes, and provisions—had to be hand-carried up the steep, narrow steel stairs. The structure is solid, but that cab gets smaller every time you look up! Fortunately, the weather was absolutely great for our deployment.

Looking up at our home for a few hours. The structure is solid, but the cab shrinks the closer you get.
100-Foot Takeoff: The view from the top is breathtaking, overlooking nothing but dense Missouri forest.

The Antenna & Operation

For the antenna, we suspended my homebrew 10m-40m linked dipole directly from the top cab structure of the tower, dropping the legs down as an inverted-V. The sheer elevation meant we had an incredible, unobstructed takeoff angle over the Ozark canopy, allowing our low-power signals to punch far above their weight class.

Operating from a massive metal frame presents unique challenges with common-mode currents traveling back down the feedline. Thankfully, the FT-240-31 common-mode toroidal choke on the linked dipole did its job perfectly, keeping the RF out of our logging equipment and audio lines. Running entirely on battery power (Class 1B) kept our noise floor virtually nonexistent.

The operating position: Yaesu FT-857 running on clean LiFePO4 battery power.
Racking up contacts down in the log book as the bands shifted into the evening.

It was a weekend of solid operating, great camaraderie, and an excellent exercise in remote emergency communications deployment. We proved we can establish a highly effective, long-range HF station anywhere—even 100 feet in the air on a moment's notice.

The Setup & Location

Operating Class: 1B (Battery / Portable)

Setting up a station at the top of a historic 100-foot watchtower requires careful planning. Every piece of gear—the Yaesu FT-857, heavy Bioenno LiFePO4 batteries, coax, ropes, and provisions—had to be hand-carried up the steep, narrow steel stairs.

For the antenna, we suspended my homebrew 10m-40m linked dipole directly from the top cab structure of the tower, dropping the legs down as an inverted-V. The sheer elevation meant we had an incredible, unobstructed takeoff angle over the Ozark canopy, allowing our QRP and low-power signals to punch far above their weight class.
Looking up at the 100-foot watchtower platform.

Key Takeaways & Lessons

Operating from a metal tower presents unique challenges. Common-mode currents can easily travel back down the feedline when you're suspended in the air like that. Thankfully, the FT-240-31 common-mode toroidal choke on the linked dipole did its job perfectly, keeping the RF out of our logging equipment and audio lines.

Running on battery power kept our noise floor incredibly low. Far away from city grid interference and elevated above local terrain, we were pulling in weak signals on 20m and 40m that would have been completely lost in the static back at a typical home station.

It was a weekend of solid operating, great camaraderie, and a reminder of why emergency communications practice is so vital. We proved we can establish a highly effective, long-range HF station anywhere—even 100 feet in the air on a moment's notice.
